00 Prologue

Read across civilization. Practice intelligence directly.

This curriculum expands an eleven-month outline into a twelve-month program that keeps the original reading spine intact while reorganizing the work into a clearer arc: foundation, expansion, integration, and synthesis. The plan treats intelligence not as a single score but as a network of abilities — verbal comprehension, working memory, processing speed, visual-spatial reasoning, social cognition, metacognition, creativity, embodied skill, language, music, rhetoric, and AI-era judgment. That shift reflects contemporary intelligence assessment, which has moved toward multidimensional models rather than reliance on a single global score Kaufman et al..

The organizing principle is simple: read across civilization, but practice intelligence directly. Each month combines historical, mathematical, artistic, and philosophical readings with exercises designed around cognitive mechanisms that are known to matter for durable learning: spaced retrieval, interleaving, deliberate practice, cognitive load management, metacognitive calibration, and transfer. Spacing and retrieval are among the strongest general-purpose learning strategies, but learners often underuse them because they feel less fluent than rereading or massed practice Carpenter, Pan & Butler. Interleaving is introduced only after initial blocked acquisition, because desirable difficulty becomes counterproductive when working memory is overloaded Paas et al..

Design stance

The purpose is not to "raise IQ" in a simplistic way. IQ-style pre/post testing can be retained as one data point, but the curriculum should track a profile of abilities. Cognitive abilities are relatively stable at the individual-rank level, especially in adulthood, so a single IQ retest should be interpreted cautiously and alongside domain-specific evidence of growth Breit et al.. Composite intelligence scores can also mask the profile of strengths and weaknesses that this curriculum is explicitly trying to develop Bünger et al..

02 Practice

The Weekly Operating System

The original outline is reading-rich and challenge-driven. The upgraded plan adds a weekly operating system so the cognitive benefits come from disciplined practice rather than exposure alone.

The retrieval notebook

Use a single notebook or digital equivalent throughout the year. Each reading session ends with a closed-book retrieval pass. Write from memory before consulting the text. Then correct in a second color or with explicit "missed," "distorted," and "invented" tags. This makes memory visible, which is essential because metacognitive judgment is often unreliable unless it is tied to actual performance data Foster et al..

Each entry should include:

  • Claims: five to ten remembered claims from the reading.
  • Terms: unfamiliar words, names, schools, technical concepts, dates, or symbols.
  • Relations: at least three links to prior readings, artworks, mathematical ideas, or philosophical positions.
  • Questions: one literal, one inferential, and one adversarial question.
  • Prediction: expected score on a later quiz or recall test.
  • Correction: actual score and what changed in the next study session.

The review schedule

Review each new item at expanding intervals: same day, 1 day, 3 days, 7 days, 14 days, 30 days. Adaptive spaced repetition can improve efficiency by adjusting intervals based on recall success, especially when learners already know some material Wilschut et al.. The spaced system should include vocabulary, philosopher positions, artworks, mathematical periods, historical transitions, and personal performance goals.

AI-use rules

AI is useful in the later curriculum, but it should not replace effortful practice. Generative AI can improve exploration and idea generation, but it introduces risks of inaccuracy, bias, automation dependence, and reduced metacognitive agency Wang et al. Romeo & Conti Zhang et al..

House rules

  • AI-free first pass: all recall, first drafts, translations, and argument outlines begin without AI.
  • AI as adversary: after the first pass, ask AI to challenge claims, generate counterarguments, or identify gaps.
  • AI as object of critique: at least once per month, evaluate an AI-generated summary against the primary text.
  • Human voice lock: final essays and speeches must be rewritten in the learner's own voice.

03 Measurement

Assessment Architecture

The original IQ-test challenge is preserved, but the upgraded curriculum treats it as one baseline measure, not the controlling goal. Contemporary intelligence research supports profile-based assessment because broad cognitive abilities do not reduce cleanly to a single practical training target McGrew et al.. Executive functions also overlap with intelligence while retaining distinct variance, especially in planning, inhibition, and fluency van Aken et al..

01

Phase 01 · Foundation

Beginnings, Baseline, and the Machinery of Recall

Readings

HistoryJ. M. Roberts, The New History of the World, Book 1, "Beginnings"
MathematicsD. E. Smith, History of Mathematics, Chapter 1, "Prehistory"
Art HistoryMarilyn Stokstad, Art History, Chapter 1, "Prehistoric Art"

Why this month belongs first

Month 1 should establish how the learner currently reads, remembers, visualizes, reasons, speaks, and recovers from distraction. It also introduces the retrieval notebook, because spaced retrieval is the core practice that makes the reading sequence cumulative rather than merely sequential Carpenter, Pan & Butler.

Baseline profile week

  • IQ-style baseline: take a standardized test in a controlled setting. Record time of day, sleep, caffeine, environment, interruptions, and subjective effort. Do not treat the result as an identity claim.
  • Reading baseline: read five pages from Roberts, close the book, and write a 10-minute free recall. Score for factual accuracy, sequence, and causal links.
  • Visual baseline: study one prehistoric artwork for three minutes. Hide it, sketch from memory, annotate what was structurally wrong.
  • Working-memory baseline: perform a reading-span or operation-span task. Record accuracy and failure point.
  • Processing-speed baseline: complete a Stroop-style or Trail Making-style task. Record speed and errors.
  • Voice baseline: record a two-minute explanation of "what makes humans symbol-making animals?" Watch it once and note clarity, pace, posture, and filler words.

Weekly practice structure

  • Week 1 — baseline & setup: build the retrieval notebook, complete baseline tasks, define language and instrument choices.
  • Week 2 — origins & evidence: create a timeline from early human symbolic life to early mathematical marks. Use three colors: material survival, symbolic survival, and inferred cognition.
  • Week 3 — visual memory: choose five prehistoric artifacts. For each, practice observe-hide-sketch-explain.
  • Week 4 — synthesis: write a 1,200-word essay, "What counts as intelligence before writing?", citing history, mathematics, and art.

Micro-drills

  • Vocabulary ladder: 40 terms sorted into material culture, cognition, social organization, and representation.
  • Analogy drill: ten analogies connecting prehistoric art to mathematical notation.
  • One-minute recall: every other day, define a prior term in one minute without notes.
  • Spatial reconstruction: redraw the same artwork three times at increasing delay (immediate, 24h, 7 days).

Monthly output

A baseline dossier: test results, first reading recall, first visual recall, first speech recording, and the first essay. The starting artifact against which Month 12 will be judged.

02

Phase 01 · Foundation

Greek Form, Argument, and the Start of Language

Readings

MathematicsSmith, Chapter 2, "Historic to 1000 BC"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapter 4, "Art of the Ancient Aegean"; Chapter 5, "Art of Ancient Greece"
PhilosophyThales in Anglin and Lambek, The Heritage of Thales; Pythagoras in Guthrie, The Pythagorean Sourcebook; Heraclitus, Fragments; Parmenides in Geldard; Xenophon's Conversations of Socrates; Plato, Republic; Aristotle, Metaphysics

Why this month belongs here

Month 2 introduces philosophical abstraction alongside Greek art and early mathematics. This is the right place to start a language because the learner is already confronting translation, categories, grammar-like logic, and unfamiliar conceptual systems. Adult second-language learning benefits from deliberate, goal-directed practice with explicit monitoring and frequent production Wedlock & Binnie.

Language launch

Choose one language and commit through Month 12. The goal is cumulative pressure on auditory discrimination, working memory, retrieval fluency, and social communication.

  • Daily SRS: 20 new or reviewed items per day, always with sentence context.
  • Production from day one: five original sentences daily using current vocabulary.
  • Listening loop: 10 min/day of comprehensible audio until segmentation improves.
  • Pronunciation: twice weekly, record and compare against a native model.
  • Monthly target: sustain a scripted two-minute self-introduction by Month 2's end.

Greek argument matrix

Build a matrix across Thales, Pythagoras, Heraclitus, Parmenides, Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle with five columns: what is real?, what is knowable?, what counts as order?, an artistic parallel, and a mathematical parallel.

Dialogue reconstruction

Once per week, reconstruct a philosophical argument as a three-voice dialogue — Teacher states the thesis, Student asks for clarification, Adversary raises a serious objection. Structured argumentation is one of the active-learning formats associated with critical-thinking gains Hamzanwadi review.

Geometry and body

Trace geometric relationships with the hand, walk the outline of a triangle or circle, and explain the relationship aloud. Gesture can support mathematical learning by linking symbolic concepts to sensorimotor structure Seccia & Goldin-Meadow.

Monthly output

Give a five-minute recorded explanation: "Why does Greek thought make form intelligent?" Include one philosopher, one artwork, and one mathematical idea. Deliver once from notes and once without notes.

03

Phase 01 · Foundation

First Civilizations, Egypt, Al-Kindi, Hafiz, and the Start of Music

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 2, "The First Civilizations"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 3, "1000 BC to 300 BC"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapter 3, "Art of Ancient Egypt"
Philosophy & literatureJennifer Viegas, Al Kindi; Hafiz, The Gift

Why this month belongs here

Music adds auditory sequencing, motor learning, timing, and embodied pattern. Instrumental music training shows small but meaningful cognitive and academic benefits, though well-controlled evidence remains limited and effects should not be overclaimed Román-Caballero et al.. Music training can also support working-memory development, especially central executive demands such as backward span Nie et al..

Music launch

Choose one instrument. The goal by Month 12 is not virtuosity; it is disciplined sensorimotor intelligence.

  • Technique block: 10 min/session — posture, hand position, breath, bowing, embouchure, or fingering.
  • Rhythm block: 10 min — metronome, clapping, counting, subdivisions.
  • Pitch or tone: 10 min — scales, intervals, tone production, tuning.
  • Repertoire: 10–15 min on one simple piece.
  • Reflection: 3 min noting friction, next step, and one micro-goal.

Civilization systems map

For each early civilization, map five systems — water and agriculture; writing and administration; mathematics and measurement; ritual and cosmology; art and power — and identify the cognitive load each system imposes.

Egyptian visual grammar & Al-Kindi crossover

For three Egyptian works: sketch from observation, label conventions, reconstruct from memory, and explain what each work is designed to make memorable. Then write a short note on Al-Kindi — philosopher, scientist, calligrapher, musician — as a model of polymathic intelligence as coordinated transfer rather than shallow dabbling.

Monthly output

Record a first musical benchmark: one scale or technical pattern, one rhythm exercise, and one short piece. Store beside Month 12's final recording.

Phase 02 · Attention & Tradition

Near East, China, the Tao, Confucius, and Attention Training

Readings

MathematicsSmith, Chapter 4, "300 BC to 500 AD"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapter 2, "Art of the Ancient Near East"
PhilosophyLao Tzu, Tao Te Ching; Confucius, The Analects

Why this month belongs here

Mindfulness-based interventions show small-to-moderate cognitive benefits across global cognition, executive attention, working memory accuracy, inhibition, shifting, and sustained attention, though effects vary by population and practice design Newman & Zainal. Brief mindfulness before a learning session can also function as a cognitive primer Li et al..

Meditation ramp

  • Week 1: 10 min/day, focused attention on breath.
  • Week 2: 20 min/day, focused attention plus distraction labeling.
  • Week 3: 30–45 min/session, four sessions minimum.
  • Week 4: one 60-minute session plus normal shorter practices.

Attention log & Confucian role intelligence

After each session record duration, type, remembered distractions, dominant emotional tone, recovery speed, and effect on the next reading session. Then build a Confucian role map — ruler, minister, parent, child, friend, student, teacher — listing expected conduct, cognitive demand, failure mode, and modern analogue. Social-cognition training can improve theory of mind, emotion recognition, and perspective-taking across the healthy lifespan Martin et al..

Taoist inversion exercise

For five strong claims from earlier philosophers, write a Taoist inversion. Example: "If X prizes explicit order, the Taoist response is…" This builds conceptual flexibility and forces non-default framing.

Monthly output

A 1,500-word essay: "Intelligence as control versus intelligence as attunement." Include Confucius, Lao Tzu, one mathematical development, and one Near Eastern artwork.

Phase 02 · Attention & Tradition

Classical Mediterranean, Rome, Skepticism, Stoicism, and Oratory

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 3, "The Classical Mediterranean"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 5, "500 AD to 1000 AD"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapter 6, "Etruscan and Roman Art"
PhilosophyEpicurus, The Essential Epicurus; Epictetus, Enchiridion; Cicero, Selected Works; Marcus Aurelius, Meditations; Sextus Empiricus, Outlines of Scepticism

Why this month belongs here

By Month 5 the learner has already practiced reading recall, philosophical comparison, meditation, language, and music. Oratory joins memory, processing speed, emotional regulation, posture, audience modeling, and argument structure.

The expanded oratory challenge

Complete at least ten recorded speeches, no more than two on a given day, across four formats: Recitation, Explanation, Argument, and Impersonation.

Six-criterion oratory rubric
CriterionQuestion
ComprehensionDid the speech actually understand the source?
ArrangementWas there a clear opening, sequence, and close?
VoiceWere pace, emphasis, silence, and volume controlled?
EmbodimentDid posture and movement support meaning?
Audience modelWas the listener's likely confusion anticipated?
Emotional controlDid intensity serve the idea rather than replace it?

Stoic control audit, skepticism drill, speech memory ladder

Keep a seven-day Stoic log of what happened, what was under control, and what judgment converted event into disturbance. Apply Stoic, Epicurean, Skeptical, and Roman civic responses to one confident weekly claim. Memorize a one-minute passage at expanding intervals — immediate, 1 day, 3 days, 7 days; spaced retrieval should be used for performance as well as factual knowledge Carpenter, Pan & Butler.

Monthly output

An oratory reel: three best clips, one worst clip, and a written self-critique explaining improvement across the month.

Phase 02 · Attention & Tradition

Diverging Traditions, Medieval Worlds, Comparative Theology, and Objective Writing

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 4, "The Age of Diverging Traditions"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 6, "Occident from 1000 AD to 1500 AD"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapters 7–14 (Jewish, Early Christian, Byzantine, Islamic, South & Southeast Asian, Chinese, Korean, Japanese, American, African, and Early Medieval European art)
PhilosophyAugustine, City of God; Boethius, Consolation of Philosophy; Anselm, Cur Deus Homo; Abelard via Marenbon; Aquinas via McInerny; Duns Scotus, A Treatise on God as First Principle; Ockham via Keele

Why this month belongs here

This is the first major overload month. The upgrade is to protect against overload using structured comparison and worked examples before open-ended essay writing. Cognitive-load theory predicts that novices struggle when too many interacting elements are introduced at once, so this month requires templates, matrices, and staged synthesis before independent argument Paas et al..

Week-by-week build

  • Week 1: one-page neutral summaries of Augustine and Boethius.
  • Week 2: concept matrix for Anselm, Abelard, Aquinas, Scotus, and Ockham.
  • Week 3: choose two writers and write a thesis-neutral comparison map.
  • Week 4: write the full essay with no editorializing.

Objectivity constraints

  • No first-person judgment.
  • No "obviously," "clearly," or "merely."
  • Every evaluative statement must be converted into a descriptive statement.
  • Each thinker must be reconstructed in terms they would recognize.

Diverging traditions atlas, razor drill, art-comparison sprint

Build a visual atlas — one page per region — listing dominant institutions, sacred architecture, script, mathematical practice, image of authority, and memory technology. Use Ockham as a compression drill: 200 words → 50 words → one sentence → diagram. Compare one Byzantine, one Islamic, one East Asian, one African, and one Early Medieval European work, asking what kind of mind does this visual system train?

Monthly output

The objective comparative essay plus a one-page reflection on what objectivity felt like: easier, harder, distorting, clarifying, or ethically necessary.

Phase 03 · Modern Self & Argument

Renaissance, Reform, Science, Machiavelli, and Editorial Judgment

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 5, "The Making of the European Age"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 7, "Orient from 1000 AD to 1500 AD"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapters 15–19 (Romanesque, Gothic, 14th-c. European, 15th-c. Northern European, 15th-c. Italian Renaissance)
Philosophy & historyDescartes, Meditations; Luther via Bainton, Here I Stand; Galileo via Sobel, Galileo's Daughter; Machiavelli, The Prince

Why this month belongs here

The deliberate reversal after Month 6's objectivity. The cognitive target is controlled stance-shifting: the learner must know when to reconstruct, when to evaluate, and when to take responsibility for judgment.

Stance ladder

For one topic each week, write the same 300-word analysis in four modes: Neutral historian, Sympathetic defender, Hostile critic, Personal editorialist. Rhetorical practice develops argumentation, audience modeling, and verbal precision, all of which support critical thinking when paired with structured active learning Hamzanwadi review.

Cartesian doubt lab & Galileo evidence trial

Apply Descartes' method to one belief from the curriculum so far: what can be doubted, what remains, what was smuggled back in, and what a medieval scholastic vs. a modern scientist would object to. Then stage a Galileo trial with six roles — Galileo, church authority, mathematician, instrument-maker, lay observer, and historian — each making an evidence-based argument.

Monthly output

An editorial essay comparing two writers from this month, with a preface explaining your evaluative standards.

Phase 03 · Modern Self & Argument

Acceleration, Education, Enlightenment, and Voice Imitation

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 6, "The Great Acceleration"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 8, "Sixteenth Century"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapters 20–25 (16th-c. Italy, Northern Europe and Iberia, 17th-c. Europe, South & Southeast Asia after 1200, China & Korea after 1279, Japan after 1333)
PhilosophyLocke, Some Thoughts Concerning Education; Hobbes, Leviathan; Hume, Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals; Rousseau, Emile; Kant, What Is Enlightenment?

Voice contradiction challenge

Choose one philosopher. Read an additional work if feasible. Then write a short essay contradicting that philosopher using their preferred vocabulary, sentence rhythm, assumptions about audience, favorite metaphors, and argumentative structure. The essay should feel like an internal betrayal: the author's own style used against one of their conclusions.

Style fingerprint & education laboratory

Profile average sentence length, preferred abstractions, favorite contrasts, use of examples, attitude toward reader, and theological/political/epistemic assumptions. Then compare Locke and Rousseau on education and design one week of learning according to each — what is taught, forbidden, trusted to nature, imposed by discipline, and what kind of adult would emerge.

AI style audit

Ask an AI system to imitate the same philosopher. Critique the output for hallucination, generic style, missing conceptual structure, and false confidence. This supports AI-era intelligence by training independent verification and resistance to over-reliance Romeo & Conti.

Monthly output

A "voice contradiction" essay with a short afterword explaining how the imitation was constructed and where it failed.

Phase 03 · Modern Self & Argument

The End of Europe's World, Moral Modernity, Social Cognition, and Ethical Influence

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 7, "The End of the Europeans' World"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 9, "Seventeenth Century"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapters 26–28 (Americas after 1300, Pacific Cultures, Africa in the Modern Era)
PhilosophyBentham, Defence of Usury; Mill, Utilitarianism or The Subjection of Women; Kierkegaard, Either/Or; Marx, The Communist Manifesto; Nietzsche, Genealogy of Morality

Reframing the original challenge

The original "covertly steer a conversation toward a topic" exercise is psychologically interesting but ethically risky because it trains covert manipulation. The upgraded version keeps the cognitive target — social modeling and conversational steering — but makes consent and debriefing explicit.

Ethical social-cognition challenge

  • Tell your partner you are practicing conversational guidance, but withhold the topic.
  • Over 20–30 minutes attempt to create natural openings toward the target topic.
  • Do not lie, pressure, or emotionally exploit.
  • Track cues that worked: shared memory, adjacent topic, question framing, curiosity, silence, or third-party reference.
  • Debrief fully: what did they notice, if anything?

Social-cognition training has evidence for improving theory of mind, emotion recognition, and perspective-taking, but should be practiced transparently rather than as covert influence Martin et al..

Moral calculus vs. existential inwardness, conversation transcript, genealogy drill

Compare Bentham, Mill, Kierkegaard, Marx, and Nietzsche on suffering, freedom, false consciousness, the individual, and what must be transformed. Annotate one conversation transcript for topic shifts, emotional turns, opening and closing questions, projection, and missed listening. For one modern moral word — "progress," "rights," "freedom" — write a Nietzschean genealogy, a Marxist genealogy, and a utilitarian defense.

Monthly output

A social-cognition audit: one annotated conversation, one ethical reflection, and one comparative moral essay.

Phase 04 · Symbol & Synthesis

Latest Age, Modern Art, Language, Being, and Formal Systems

Readings

HistoryRoberts, Book 8, "The Latest Age"
MathematicsSmith, Chapter 10, "Eighteenth Century and After"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapters 29–31 (18th–19th-c. art, mid-late 19th-c. art, Modern Art 1900–1950)
Philosophy & systemsWittgenstein, Tractatus; Heidegger, The Principle of Reason; Sartre, No Exit; Rorty, Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature; Hofstadter, Gödel, Escher, Bach

Expanded pastiche challenge

Choose a deceased political figure from the last 100 years. Research life, writings, speeches, correspondences, and historical context. Write a fictional letter to a confidant, dated the day after their death, without acknowledging the death.

  • Include a preface clearly labeling the piece as fiction.
  • Build a source dossier of phrases, concerns, relationships, and historical pressures.
  • Capture syntax, rhythm, worldview, and blind spots.
  • Avoid anachronistic concepts.
  • Include one private fear, one unresolved political tension, and one detail only a close reader would know.

Tractatus compression, GEB loop hunt, modern-art perception lab

Produce numbered propositions (1, 1.1, 1.11, 1.12) for each major reading to train hierarchical structure and compression. Find strange loops across the year — self-reference in philosophy, recursion in mathematics, mirror structures in art, feedback in history, imitation loops in rhetoric, AI as a mirror of human language. For five modern artworks, do a three-pass analysis: pre-interpretation, after historical context, and after philosophical framing.

Monthly output

The fictional letter plus a process note explaining how voice, evidence, and invention were separated.

Phase 04 · Symbol & Synthesis

International Scene, Modern Mathematics, Reassessment, and Transfer Testing

Readings

MathematicsSmith, Chapter 10, "Theoretical Modern Mathematics"
Art HistoryStokstad, Chapter 32, "The International Scene Since 1950"

Why Month 11 is not the end

Month 11 becomes reassessment; Month 12 becomes interpretation and publication. This avoids the trap of testing and stopping before the learner has synthesized what the test did not measure.

Reassessment protocol

Repeat the original IQ-style test as closely as possible — same time of day, similar sleep, food, caffeine, environment, device, timing, and no warm-up that was not used at baseline. Then add domain-specific reassessments: novel reading recall, novel visual recall, mental rotation, working-memory span, processing speed, a language recording, a music recording, a five-minute speech, an AI critique task, and a social-cognition vignette.

Transfer battery

The key question is not "did practiced tasks improve?" but "what transfers to novel material?" Transfer is most reliable when the new task shares mechanisms with the trained task, while far transfer from working-memory training to broad fluid intelligence is consistently weak Zając-Lamparska Lau et al.. Use novel tasks:

  • Summarize an unfamiliar philosophical passage.
  • Rotate an unfamiliar object.
  • Learn 20 new words in the target language and test after 48 hours.
  • Sight-read or rhythm-read unfamiliar music.
  • Critique an AI summary of a text never studied.
  • Give a speech on a topic assigned 10 minutes earlier.

International scene synthesis

Choose three post-1950 artworks from different regions. For each, describe the work formally, connect it to political or technological modernity, identify the perception it demands, and relate it to one philosophical idea from Month 10.

Monthly output

A reassessment report with charts or tables comparing Month 1, Month 6, and Month 11/12 performance. Interpret cautiously — improvement on practiced tasks is meaningful, but it is not the same as global intelligence change.

12

Phase 04 · Symbol & Synthesis

Capstone, AI-Era Intelligence, Portfolio, and Public Synthesis

Purpose

Month 12 is the missing synthesis month. It converts the year into a publishable intellectual artifact and asks the learner to demonstrate intelligence as coordination: memory, perception, argument, language, music, ethics, style, and tool judgment working together.

Capstone theme — choose one

  • Intelligence and civilization: how societies externalize cognition through writing, art, mathematics, institutions, and machines.
  • The history of abstraction: from marks, myths, and measures to formal systems and AI.
  • The trained mind: a first-person account of how practice changes attention, memory, taste, and judgment.
  • Symbol, body, and machine: intelligence as embodied action, cultural memory, and computational extension.
  • Mysticism, reason, and pattern: the relationship between disciplined attention, symbolic systems, and experiences of meaning.

05 Drills

The Exercise Library

A reference set of drills the curriculum draws on. Mix and match within any month; never rely on exposure alone.

Retrieval & memory

  • Closed-book recallRead, close, write, correct.
  • Cued recallUse only chapter headings as prompts.
  • Free recall mapDraw a concept map from memory, then repair it.
  • Name–date–idea triadsFor each figure: one date range, one idea, one opposition.
  • Delayed reconstructionReconstruct an argument after 24 hours and again after 7 days.

Verbal comprehension

  • Feynman paragraphExplain a dense passage to a bright teenager.
  • Adversarial summarySummarize so well that a defender would accept it.
  • Vocabulary genealogyTrace a technical word across three authors.
  • Analogy ladderBuild analogies from concrete to abstract.
  • Compression drill500 → 100 → 25 words → one sentence.

Visual-spatial reasoning

  • Observe-hide-sketchDraw an artwork or diagram from memory.
  • Mental rotationRotate imagined objects, maps, and diagrams.
  • Spatial narrationVerbally describe the route through an image.
  • Reverse compositionInfer how a work was constructed from its final arrangement.
  • Multi-perspective planRedraw from front, side, and top views.

Working memory

  • Reading spanRead sentences and recall final words in sequence.
  • Operation spanSolve simple operations while remembering words or symbols.
  • Argument holdKeep three premises in mind while generating objections.
  • Language holdHold a target-language sentence while answering a question.
  • Music holdRemember a short melodic phrase while identifying intervals.

Processing speed

  • Timed classificationSort concepts under time pressure without sacrificing accuracy.
  • Stroop variationName ink color, then semantic category, then emotional tone.
  • Task switchingAlternate between summarizing and critiquing every two minutes.
  • Rapid translationTranslate or paraphrase short sentences against a timer.
  • Physical primer5–10 minutes of brisk movement before timed cognitive work.

Creativity

  • Alternate usesGenerate uses for an object, then score for originality.
  • Counterfactual historyChange one historical variable and reason downstream.
  • Metaphor forgeCreate metaphors linking math, art, and philosophy.
  • Narrative causalityTurn abstract argument into a causal story.
  • Constraint compositionWrite under strict formal constraints, then revise freely.

Social intelligence

  • Steelman debateDefend the view you reject.
  • Role reversalExplain a conflict from every participant's perspective.
  • Conversation annotationMark openings, closures, emotional pivots, assumptions.
  • Bias auditIdentify anchoring, availability, attribution error, curse of knowledge.
  • Collaborative synthesisAssign roles: builder, critic, historian, summarizer.

AI-era intelligence

  • AI hallucination huntCompare an AI summary to the primary text.
  • Source provenance auditTrace every factual claim to a source.
  • Prompt as rhetoricRewrite prompts as claims, warrants, and constraints.
  • Human-first draftWrite before AI assistance, then compare.
  • Model disagreementAsk two systems the same question and adjudicate differences.

07 Form

Practical Guidance for Web Publishing

The content is intellectually dense, so the design should reduce cognitive load. A restrained editorial layout is the right choice.

  • Navigation: persistent table of contents with phase anchors.
  • Monthly cards: title, readings, targets, exercises, output.
  • Callouts: used sparingly for principles, cautions, and practice rules.
  • Tables: for timelines, comparisons, assessments, and rubrics.
  • Media embeds: speech clips, music benchmarks, sketches, and capstone slides — only where they demonstrate growth.
  • Typography: a readable serif or sans for body; a strong editorial display face for headings.
  • Color: one accent color for phase labels and links; avoid decorative palettes.
  • Accessibility: body text at 16px or larger, strong contrast, captions for media, alt text for images.

08 Coda

Final Notes on the Curriculum's Philosophy

The original outline understood something important: intelligence is not trained by puzzles alone. It is trained by moving between symbolic systems, inhabiting alien viewpoints, remembering difficult sequences, arguing in public, learning with the body, and returning to old material after forgetting has begun. Modern learning science supports that general direction but adds sharper constraints. Retrieval matters more than rereading, interleaving must be timed carefully, working-memory training transfers only when mechanisms overlap, and AI assistance must not replace unassisted effort Carpenter, Pan & Butler Paas et al. Zając-Lamparska Zhang et al..

The best version of this curriculum is therefore not a promise of transformation by exposure. It is a year-long apprenticeship in disciplined attention: attention to texts, images, sounds, bodies, other minds, machines, and the learner's own errors. If the curriculum succeeds, the final evidence will not be only a score. It will be a portfolio showing that the learner can remember more accurately, perceive more carefully, speak more persuasively, practice more deliberately, use tools more skeptically, and synthesize more generously across the long record of human intelligence.
